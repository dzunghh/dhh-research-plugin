## 1. Sex differences in cancer incidence among people with HIV infection in the United States — Score 6/6

- **Journal**: J Natl Cancer Inst
- **Pub date**: 2026-06-12
- **Authors**: Maclin BJ et al.
- **Aims**: Examine whether sex differences in cancer incidence across 20 sites are attenuated among immunocompromised adults living with HIV.
- **Method**: Population-based HIV–cancer registry linkage study, 2001–2019, 3,956,838 person-years; Poisson regression adjusting for age, race/ethnicity, calendar year, HIV transmission category.
- **Key findings**: Male-to-female incidence ratio was significantly attenuated for 9 cancers (tongue, oropharynx, esophagus, colon, liver, larynx, lung, bladder, DLBCL) in people with HIV vs. general population. Hodgkin lymphoma showed the opposite pattern (M:F IRR 1.96 vs. 1.45, P=0.0001).
- **Strengths**: Largest sex-stratified cancer incidence study in immunocompromised adults; 20 cancer sites; multi-state registry linkage with ~4M person-years.
- **Limitations**: Cannot separate behavioral from immune-mediated explanations; HIV treatment era heterogeneity not fully captured.
- **Conclusion**: Immune differences between sexes in the general population appear to contribute to sex disparities in cancer incidence beyond lifestyle factors.
- **ID**: DOI:10.1093/jnci/djag185 / PMID:42286371

## 2. Utility of recurrence-based surrogate endpoints for survival in adjuvant cancer trials: evidence from melanoma — Score 6/6

- **Journal**: J Natl Cancer Inst
- **Pub date**: 2026-06-15
- **Authors**: Orme SE et al.
- **Aims**: Formally validate DFS and DMFS as surrogate endpoints for disease-specific and overall survival in resected melanoma to guide adjuvant trial design.
- **Method**: 10,379 AJCC stage I–III melanoma patients from 5 international centres; trial-level surrogacy assessed by restricted mean survival time and Kendall's τ at 12–48 month horizons.
- **Key findings**: DFS showed strong trial-level surrogacy for DSS at 24–36 months (R² up to 0.95); DMFS consistently high (R² 0.87–0.91); OS correlated poorly at all horizons (R² <0.40). Surrogacy was robust in pT-defined IIB–IIC but limited in IB–IIA disease.
- **Strengths**: 10,379 patients across 5 international centres; rigorous RMST-based framework; pre-specified stage subgroup analyses.
- **Limitations**: Observational multi-centre cohort, not randomised; may not capture treatment-effect heterogeneity across novel immunotherapy regimens.
- **Conclusion**: DFS and DMFS are valid surrogates for DSS in high-risk resected melanoma; OS performs poorly at early horizons, limiting its utility as primary endpoint.
- **ID**: DOI:10.1093/jnci/djag183 / PMID:42295855

## 3. Evaluating statistical models for overdispersed multi-omics data: a multiplex immunofluorescence case study — Score 6/6

- **Journal**: Am J Epidemiol
- **Pub date**: 2026-06-15
- **Authors**: Thomas CE et al.
- **Aims**: Identify appropriate statistical models for cancer-epi studies analysing overdispersed, zero-inflated multi-omic count data.
- **Method**: 1,235 colorectal cancer cases with multiplex immunofluorescence T-cell density data; seven models compared (ordinal logistic, Poisson, quasi-Poisson, quadratic/linear NB, ZINB, hurdle NB); simulation studies of type I error and power.
- **Key findings**: Poisson and standard NB-based models showed inflated false-positive rates. Ordinal logistic, linear NB, and quasi-Poisson models achieved best or near-best power with controlled type I error across simulated conditions.
- **Strengths**: Dual real-data and simulation validation; directly translatable guidance using 1,235 real CRC cases; covers diverse zero proportions and dispersion levels.
- **Limitations**: Single cancer type and assay platform; generalisability to other multi-omic modalities requires further evaluation.
- **Conclusion**: Ordinal logistic, linear NB, or quasi-Poisson models are recommended for overdispersed multi-omic data in cancer epidemiology.
- **ID**: DOI:10.1093/aje/kwag127 / PMID:42294596

## 4. Modeling long-term mortality and morbidity in pediatric Hodgkin lymphoma survivors after reduced radiotherapy exposure — Score 5/6

- **Journal**: J Natl Cancer Inst
- **Pub date**: 2026-06-12
- **Authors**: Rogers JR et al.
- **Aims**: Project overall survival, breast cancer, and heart failure cumulative incidence to age 65 in 5-year pediatric HL survivors under historical treatment regimens.
- **Method**: Simulation model parameterised from Childhood Cancer Survivor Study and national databases; compared extended-field RT, chest RT ≥35 Gy, chest RT <35 Gy, and chemotherapy-only cohorts.
- **Key findings**: Projected OS at age 65 ranged from 24.7% (extended-field RT) to 71.8% (chemotherapy only) vs. 86.4% general population. Cumulative breast cancer incidence was 61.1% (extended-field RT) vs. 6.1% (general population); heart failure 34.8% vs. 4.3%.
- **Strengths**: Validated model using nationally representative survivor study data; projects risks to late adulthood (age 65) previously unquantified.
- **Limitations**: Uncertainty intervals are wide at age 65; modern RT techniques post-2005 not fully represented; competing risks handling limited.
- **Conclusion**: Even with reduced radiotherapy, pediatric HL survivors face markedly elevated breast cancer and heart failure risks well into adulthood, supporting lifelong surveillance.
- **ID**: DOI:10.1093/jnci/djag186 / PMID:42286308

## 5. Can ovarian cancer screening work? A secondary analysis of the UK Collaborative Trial of Ovarian Cancer Screening — Score 5/6

- **Journal**: Br J Cancer
- **Pub date**: 2026-06-13
- **Authors**: Lange JM et al.
- **Aims**: Estimate the natural history of HGSC using UKCTOCS data and simulate conditions required for effective ovarian cancer screening.
- **Method**: Individual-level screening and diagnosis data from the UKCTOCS multimodal screening arm; natural history model fitted to HGSC; simulated extended and improved screening scenarios.
- **Key findings**: Estimated detectable window for HGSC under current MMS was <6 months; continued screening yielded at most 15% relative mortality reduction. Achieving ≥20% mortality reduction required extending the detectable window to ≥1 year plus ≥70% sensitivity.
- **Strengths**: Uses real individual-level UKCTOCS data; ties natural history modeling directly to trial-observed outcomes; provides specific benchmark sensitivity and window requirements.
- **Limitations**: Single RCT dataset; model assumes stable natural history; heterogeneous HGSC subtypes not distinguished.
- **Conclusion**: Current screening modalities cannot intercept HGSC early enough; effective screening will require first- or second-line tests that substantially extend the detectable early-stage window.
- **ID**: DOI:10.1038/s41416-026-03490-2 / PMID:42288686

## Appraisal notes

This week's digest is dominated by epi-methodology contributions of unusually high quality: formal surrogate-endpoint validation for adjuvant oncology trials (JNCI, 10,379 patients) and a rigorous model-comparison paper for multi-omic count data (AmJEpi, 1,235 CRC cases plus simulations) both address reproducibility gaps that affect many downstream studies. Together, the five papers reflect a maturing emphasis on methodological rigour, long-term survivorship quantification, and biologically grounded sex-stratified epidemiology.
