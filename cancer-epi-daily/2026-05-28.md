## 1. CHIP × PM2.5 forms a gene–environment interaction driving NSCLC in non-smoking individuals — Score 6/6

- **Journal**: Journal of the National Cancer Institute
- **Pub date**: 2026-05-27
- **Authors**: Buttigieg MM, Vlasschaert C, Pershad Y, Bick AG et al.
- **Aims**: Determine whether clonal hematopoiesis of indeterminate potential (CHIP) synergises with PM2.5 air pollution to elevate non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) risk in never-smokers.
- **Method**: Genetic, environmental, and phenotypic data from >650,000 UK Biobank and All of Us participants; meta-analysis of Cox PH models; formal interaction test; inflammatory marker analysis (CRP, IL-6, IL-1β).
- **Key findings**: CHIP alone associated with HR=1.76 (95% CI 1.07–2.89) for NSCLC in never-smokers; CHIP × above-median PM2.5 interaction HR=2.51 (95% CI 1.55–4.05; p-interaction=0.02); CHIP × PM2.5 co-exposure associated with elevated systemic inflammatory markers.
- **Strengths**: Two large independent biobanks with replication; formal G×E interaction test; biologically coherent mechanism (hyperinflammatory macrophages linking CHIP and inhaled PM2.5 at the air–lung interface).
- **Limitations**: Observational; CHIP ascertainment across platforms may differ; directionality of inflammation (cause vs consequence) requires further investigation.
- **Conclusion**: CHIP and PM2.5 form a novel somatic gene × environment interaction that may explain excess NSCLC incidence in never-smokers, identifying a potentially screenable high-risk subgroup.
- **ID**: DOI:10.1093/jnci/djag159 / PMID:42202166

## 2. Rogaratinib (pan-FGFR inhibitor) in SDH-deficient gastrointestinal stromal tumours: phase 2 results — Score 5/6

- **Journal**: Nature Medicine
- **Pub date**: 2026-05-26
- **Authors**: Merriam P, Morrow JJ, George S, Bernstein B et al.
- **Aims**: Evaluate the objective response rate of the pan-FGFR inhibitor rogaratinib in patients with advanced SDH-deficient GIST.
- **Method**: Phase 2 single-arm trial (NCT04595747); n=24 SDH-deficient GIST patients; primary endpoint ORR; serial biopsies for WES and pharmacodynamic analyses; PK/PD exploration.
- **Key findings**: ORR 41.7% (10/24 partial responses); median PFS 31.0 months (95% CI 20.2–NR); 1-year PFS 77.4% (95% CI 61.7–97.1%); toxicities manageable (hyperphosphatemia confirms FGFR1 target engagement).
- **Strengths**: First prospective trial in this epigenetically defined subgroup; rational mechanistic basis (hypermethylation → insulator disruption → FGF3/4 autocrine activation); impressive PFS in an otherwise imatinib-refractory disease.
- **Limitations**: Small n=24 single-arm; rare tumour type; no randomised comparator.
- **Conclusion**: Rogaratinib demonstrates meaningful activity in SDH-deficient GIST, providing proof-of-concept that epigenetically driven oncogene activation is a druggable target class.
- **ID**: DOI:10.1038/s41591-026-04376-9 / PMID:42191879

## 3. BRIDGE: a deconvolution framework for neoadjuvant breast cancer response prediction — Score 5/6

- **Journal**: Annals of Oncology
- **Pub date**: 2026-05-26
- **Authors**: Cantore T, Hoang DT, Nair NU, Ruppin E et al. (NCI Cancer Data Science Lab)
- **Aims**: Develop and validate a biologically grounded computational framework (BRIDGE) to predict pathologic complete response to neoadjuvant therapy in breast cancer.
- **Method**: Transcriptome deconvolution to estimate molecular subtype composition; trained on 10 and tested on 24 independent datasets spanning ER+/HER2−, HER2+, and TNBC; BRIDGE-Slide applies deep learning to pre-treatment H&E slides; spatial transcriptomics validation.
- **Key findings**: AUC 0.84 (ER+/HER2−, OR=8), 0.77 (HER2+, OR=8.3), 0.73 (TNBC, OR=3.1); outperforms Oncotype DX, MammaPrint, and ROR-S; BRIDGE-Slide performance competitive with RNA-based predictions.
- **Strengths**: 34 total training + validation datasets; biologically interpretable subtype-based deconvolution; histopathology version (BRIDGE-Slide) is clinically practical as a fast, low-cost tool.
- **Limitations**: Retrospective; BRIDGE-Slide requires prospective validation; spatial cohort small; external generalisability to regimens beyond those represented is exploratory.
- **Conclusion**: BRIDGE provides a broadly validated, biologically grounded biomarker for neoadjuvant breast cancer response; the H&E-based variant opens a low-cost clinical path pending prospective testing.
- **ID**: DOI:10.1016/j.annonc.2026.05.700 / PMID:42203036

## 4. Longitudinal suicidal self-directed violence risk among US veterans with cancer — Score 5/6

- **Journal**: JAMA Oncology
- **Pub date**: 2026-05-28
- **Authors**: Sullivan DR, Disher N, Nugent SM et al.
- **Aims**: Characterise longitudinal rates, methods, temporal trajectories, and risk factors for suicidal self-directed violence (SSDV) in veterans with cancer.
- **Method**: National cohort study (n=292,271 veterans with cancer, 2014–2023); linked VA, oncology, and suicide registries; multivariable Cox PH models; rates per 100,000 person-years; 10-year follow-up.
- **Key findings**: Overall SSDV rate 203/100,000 PY (2,400 events, 1%); highest rates: CNS cancer (394/100,000 PY), severe frailty (544), female sex (369), age ≤45 (643); Asian race aHR=2.55 vs White at 6 months post-diagnosis; SSDV risk persisted 5 years post-diagnosis for young, unmarried, CNS, and advanced-stage veterans.
- **Strengths**: Largest US cancer-suicide cohort study; captures both fatal and non-fatal attempts; 10-year longitudinal trajectory enables time-based risk characterisation; broad demographic and cancer-type stratification.
- **Limitations**: Veteran population may not generalise to non-veterans; SSDV under-recording likely in VA databases; observational design limits causal inference.
- **Conclusion**: Suicide risk in veterans with cancer persists years into survivorship; previously overlooked groups (young, Asian, thyroid cancer) require targeted routine screening as a core component of oncology care.
- **ID**: DOI:10.1001/jamaoncol.2026.1459 / PMID:42207508

## 5. Post-cancer depression and dementia risk: immortal time bias in survivorship cohorts — Score 5/6

- **Journal**: Cancer Epidemiology, Biomarkers & Prevention
- **Pub date**: 2026-05-28
- **Authors**: Lee S, Kim S
- **Aims**: Examine how the timing of post-diagnosis depression relates to all-cause dementia and Alzheimer's disease risk in cancer survivors, and compare time-fixed versus time-varying modelling approaches.
- **Method**: Korean National Health Insurance Service nationwide cohort (n=385,092 cancer survivors aged ≥55, diagnosed 2009–2013, no baseline dementia or depression; median follow-up 11.7 years); depression coded using ICD-10 F32–F33; primary outcome all-cause dementia; Cox PH models with depression modelled as both time-fixed and time-varying exposure.
- **Key findings**: Time-fixed models suggested an apparent inverse association for depression >5 years after cancer diagnosis (aHR=0.90, 95% CI 0.87–0.93); time-varying models showed consistently elevated dementia risk at all depression-onset intervals, including >5 years after cancer (aHR=2.09, 95% CI 2.02–2.17). Results similar for Alzheimer's disease.
- **Strengths**: Very large nationwide cohort; 11.7-year follow-up; direct head-to-head methodological comparison; clinical relevance for survivorship mental health programmes.
- **Limitations**: ICD-10-coded depression lacks severity grading; Korean population may limit generalisability; residual cardiometabolic confounding possible.
- **Conclusion**: Post-cancer depression is consistently associated with approximately doubled dementia risk; time-fixed analyses introduce immortal time bias that can invert the apparent direction of association, underscoring that time-varying exposure modelling is essential in survivorship research.
- **ID**: DOI:10.1158/1055-9965.EPI-26-0336 / PMID:42206989

## Appraisal notes

Three converging themes define this window. **Inflammation as a molecular hub**: the JNCI CHIP × PM2.5 interaction study (n>650,000) is the strongest new epi paper — it identifies a somatic G×E interaction explaining excess NSCLC in never-smokers through hyperinflammatory macrophage biology, and joins a growing body of work (INFLA-score, post-cancer CVD biomarkers, air pollution in CRC survivorship) framing chronic systemic inflammation as the quantifiable common pathway for multiple cancer exposures. **Precision oncology evidence generation**: the SDH-deficient GIST rogaratinib trial and the BRIDGE breast cancer prediction framework exemplify opposite ends of precision medicine — a small but impressive phase 2 converting an epigenetic mechanism into a therapy (ORR 41.7%, median PFS 31 months in a molecularly defined rare subgroup), versus a computational biomarker trained and validated at atlas scale (34 datasets) that could bring response prediction to any clinical site with an H&E scanner. **Survivorship epidemiology coming of age**: two studies published on the same date in JAMA Oncol and CEBP legitimise mental health outcomes as core cancer epidemiology — suicide risk trajectories over 10 years in a quarter-million veterans, and a methodologically instructive demonstration that immortal time bias in time-fixed models can entirely invert the apparent protective effect of late-onset post-cancer depression on dementia risk.
