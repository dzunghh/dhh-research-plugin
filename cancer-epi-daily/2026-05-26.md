## 1. Metabolic and inflammatory biomarker trajectories after a cancer diagnosis and the risk of cardiovascular diseases — Score 6/6

- **Journal**: Nature Communications
- **Pub date**: 2026-05-25
- **Authors**: Park H et al.
- **Aims**: To identify post-cancer biomarker trajectories and their associations with subsequent CVD risk in cancer patients.
- **Method**: Cohort study (1985–2020); latent class growth modelling for 11 biomarkers; Cox regression for CVD outcomes.
- **Key findings**: High/rising glucose trajectory conferred 110% higher CVD risk vs low-stable; low-stable albumin (even within normal range) elevated CVD risk; rising uric acid reduced risk ~20%.
- **Strengths**: 35-year follow-up, multi-biomarker latent trajectory approach, repeated-measures design in a large cohort.
- **Limitations**: Observational; residual confounding possible; biomarker measurement frequency not fully reported.
- **Conclusion**: Glucose, albumin, and uric acid trajectories stratify CVD risk in cancer survivors, informing targeted prevention strategies.
- **ID**: DOI:10.1038/s41467-026-73530-1 / PMID:42185299

## 2. Thyroid disease and breast cancer risk in the Sister Study — Score 5/6

- **Journal**: Cancer Epidemiology, Biomarkers & Prevention
- **Pub date**: 2026-05-26
- **Authors**: Woo JMP et al.
- **Aims**: To assess the effects of hyper- and hypothyroidism on incident breast cancer in a large prospective US cohort.
- **Method**: Prospective cohort (Sister Study, n=45,783); time-varying Cox regression; follow-up through September 2021.
- **Key findings**: Hyperthyroidism associated with 85% higher premenopausal BC risk (HR=1.85, 95% CI: 1.11–3.07); hypothyroidism showed non-significant inverse association (HR=0.75).
- **Strengths**: Large sample, time-varying exposure modelling, 18-year follow-up, stratified by menopausal status and race/ethnicity.
- **Limitations**: Self-reported thyroid disease; unmeasured thyroid hormone levels may confound estimates.
- **Conclusion**: Hyperthyroidism, especially in premenopausal women receiving treatment, may warrant enhanced breast cancer screening.
- **ID**: DOI:10.1158/1055-9965.EPI-25-1499 / PMID:42189187

## 3. Safety-Driven Response Adaptive Randomization: An Application in Noninferiority Oncology Trials — Score 5/6

- **Journal**: Statistics in Medicine
- **Pub date**: 2026-06
- **Authors**: Vittoria Chiaruttini M et al.
- **Aims**: To propose SAFER, a novel RAR design leveraging early safety data for treatment allocation in oncology noninferiority trials.
- **Method**: Novel adaptive randomization design; simulation study informed by the CAPP-IT Phase III trial; safety–efficacy endpoint correlation modelled.
- **Key findings**: SAFER preserves statistical power, reduces adverse event rates, and allows flexible adaptation speed depending on endpoint temporal alignment.
- **Strengths**: Principled handling of delayed efficacy endpoints; addresses real gap in adaptive design literature; simulation-validated.
- **Limitations**: Performance depends on assumed safety–efficacy correlation; real-world implementation complexity not fully evaluated.
- **Conclusion**: SAFER provides a safety-driven adaptive framework for oncology noninferiority trials with delayed efficacy outcomes.
- **ID**: DOI:10.1002/sim.70612 / PMID:42186850

## 4. Bayesian Sparse Regression for Microbiome-Metabolite Data Integration — Score 5/6

- **Journal**: Statistics in Medicine
- **Pub date**: 2026-06
- **Authors**: Jiang K et al.
- **Aims**: To develop a Bayesian method integrating compositional microbiome and high-missingness metabolite data for cancer risk research.
- **Method**: Bayesian regression with dual missingness mechanism modelling and compositional prior; simulation + colorectal cancer real-data application.
- **Key findings**: Model accurately imputes unobserved metabolite values and correctly selects relevant microbiome predictors; validated on colorectal cancer microbiome-metabolome dataset.
- **Strengths**: Simultaneously addresses two major challenges (metabolite missingness + microbiome compositionality) in a unified framework.
- **Limitations**: Computational scalability to large datasets not benchmarked; restricted to one cancer application.
- **Conclusion**: Bayesian sparse regression advances multi-omics integration for cancer risk and microbiome epidemiology research.
- **ID**: DOI:10.1002/sim.70607 / PMID:42186240

## 5. Machine learning model-guided selective use of temporary diverting ileostomy in rectal cancer surgery: a randomized controlled trial — Score 5/6

- **Journal**: Nature Communications
- **Pub date**: 2026-05-25
- **Authors**: Shao S et al.
- **Aims**: To evaluate whether the ML-based RTID anastomotic leakage prediction model improves ileostomy decision-making in rectal cancer surgery.
- **Method**: RCT (n=750, stage I–III rectal cancer); 1:1 randomization to surgeon discretion vs RTID-guided decisions; multi-center.
- **Key findings**: RTID reduced overall ileostomy rate (18.6% vs 40.5%, P<0.001) and unnecessary stoma formation (17.7% vs 41.3%), with comparable anastomotic leakage rates (2.4% vs 2.7%).
- **Strengths**: First RCT of ML-guided ileostomy decision; multi-center; objective quantification of unnecessary stoma reduction.
- **Limitations**: Underpowered to formally test anastomotic leakage non-inferiority; single-country (China) population limits generalizability.
- **Conclusion**: ML-guided decisions significantly reduce unnecessary stomas in rectal cancer surgery without compromising safety.
- **ID**: DOI:10.1038/s41467-026-73565-4 / PMID:42185305
