## 1. Biological aging and generational shifts in early-onset cancer risk — Score 6/6

- **Journal**: Nature Medicine
- **Pub date**: 2026-06-22
- **Authors**: Tian R et al.
- **Aims**: Determine whether accelerated biological aging explains rising early-onset solid cancer incidence across recent birth cohorts.
- **Method**: Prospective cohort study (UK Biobank n=154,169; validated in All of Us n=10,262); PhenoAge and multiple biological age clocks; Cox regression for cancer incidence.
- **Key findings**: Biological aging increased 23% SD in cohorts born 1965–1974 vs 1950–1954; PhenoAge HR 1.08 (95% CI 1.03–1.13) for early-onset solid cancer. Immune aging linked to lung cancer HR 1.89; adipose aging to colorectal cancer HR 1.60.
- **Strengths**: Multiple validated biological aging measures; two independent large cohorts; organ-specific proteomics aging analyses.
- **Limitations**: UK Biobank participation bias; only partial replication in All of Us; limited racial/ethnic diversity.
- **Conclusion**: Advanced biological aging relative to chronological age is independently associated with early-onset solid cancer risk, highlighting prevention targets.
- **ID**: DOI:10.1038/s41591-026-04448-w / PMID:42332142

## 2. Trends in colorectal cancer mortality among younger versus older adults in 49 countries — Score 5/6

- **Journal**: Journal of the National Cancer Institute
- **Pub date**: 2026-06-23
- **Authors**: Sung H et al.
- **Aims**: Characterize recent mortality trends in CRC among adults aged 25–49 versus 50–79 years across 49 countries.
- **Method**: Descriptive epidemiology using WHO Mortality Database 1990–2023; annual percent change estimation by country and age group.
- **Key findings**: CRC mortality in younger adults increased in 18/49 countries (0.7–4.3%/year); in half, older-adult rates were stable or falling. Steepest rises in Paraguay, Uruguay, Chile, and the UK.
- **Strengths**: Largest global CRC mortality trend analysis by age group; 33-year WHO database spanning 49 countries.
- **Limitations**: Mortality data completeness and coding practices vary by country, affecting cross-national comparability.
- **Conclusion**: Rising CRC mortality in younger adults across 18 countries signals a growing future burden requiring etiologic investigation and earlier detection strategies.
- **ID**: DOI:10.1093/jnci/djag153 / PMID:42331356

## 3. Comparative 10-year performance of mammography AI, polygenic, and clinical breast cancer risk models in the Kaiser Permanente Research Bank — Score 5/6

- **Journal**: Journal of the National Cancer Institute
- **Pub date**: 2026-06-23
- **Authors**: Arasu VA et al.
- **Aims**: Compare 10-year breast cancer risk discrimination of mammographic AI (Mirai), polygenic (PRS313adj), and clinical (BCSCv3) risk scores alone and combined.
- **Method**: Prospective cohort study (n=82,957 women; multiracial); Cox regression; 5-fold cross-validated C-index; 2,471 breast cancers over follow-up.
- **Key findings**: Combined model C-index 0.70 (95% CI 0.69–0.71) significantly exceeded individual scores (BCSCv3 0.62; PRS313adj 0.61; Mirai 0.66). Combination outperformed any single-domain model.
- **Strengths**: Large diverse prospective cohort; head-to-head comparison of all three risk domains; 10-year horizon.
- **Limitations**: Predominantly non-Hispanic White; single health system limits external generalizability.
- **Conclusion**: Integrating mammographic AI with polygenic and clinical risk scores significantly improves 10-year breast cancer risk discrimination, supporting combined-model personalized screening.
- **ID**: DOI:10.1093/jnci/djag154 / PMID:42331353

## 4. Stockholm3-Magnetic Resonance Imaging Population-Based Prostate Cancer Screening Study: Two-Year Follow-up — Score 4/6

- **Journal**: Annals of Internal Medicine
- **Pub date**: 2026-06-23
- **Authors**: Palsdottir T et al.
- **Aims**: Compare Stockholm3 multivariable risk score versus PSA for detecting clinically significant prostate cancer in population-based screening with 2-year follow-up.
- **Method**: Secondary analysis of STHLM3-MRI RCT (n=12,670 men aged 50–74); linkage to Swedish National Cancer Register; decision curve analysis.
- **Key findings**: Stockholm3 sensitivity 90% vs PSA 74%; false-negative rate 10% vs 26%. Decision curve analysis showed higher net benefit for Stockholm3 across biopsy-threshold range.
- **Strengths**: Population-based RCT with cancer registry linkage; decision curve analysis provides clinical utility framing.
- **Limitations**: Only 25% participation rate; 2-year follow-up; predominantly Swedish/European cohort limiting generalizability.
- **Conclusion**: Stockholm3 provides greater clinical net benefit than PSA alone for detecting clinically significant prostate cancer, driven by substantially fewer false-negative results.
- **ID**: DOI:10.7326/ANNALS-25-04753 / PMID:42330502

## 5. Tremelimumab with or without durvalumab in combination with paclitaxel in metastatic urothelial cancer: phase I/II ICRA trial — Score 4/6

- **Journal**: Nature Communications
- **Pub date**: 2026-06-17
- **Authors**: Einerhand SMH et al.
- **Aims**: Evaluate paclitaxel plus tremelimumab ± durvalumab in therapy-refractory metastatic urothelial carcinoma after platinum and ICI failure.
- **Method**: Phase I/II multicenter open-label RCT (NCT03871036; n=44); 3 arms; primary endpoint: objective response rate (88% CI); transcriptomic correlatives.
- **Key findings**: ORR 26% for paclitaxel + high-dose tremelimumab (arm A) vs 17% (arm B) and 8% (arm C). Grade 3–4 adverse events: 45%, 75%, 25% per arm.
- **Strengths**: Randomized design with pre-specified endpoints; murine and transcriptomic translational analyses supporting biological rationale.
- **Limitations**: Small total sample (n=44); open-label; non-standard 88% confidence intervals.
- **Conclusion**: Paclitaxel plus high-dose anti-CTLA-4 showed encouraging activity in therapy-refractory metastatic urothelial carcinoma; immune induction possible after anti-PD-(L)1 failure.
- **ID**: DOI:10.1038/s41467-026-74570-3 / PMID:42310313
