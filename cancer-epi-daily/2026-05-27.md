## 1. Zanidatamab with and without Tislelizumab in HER2-Positive Gastroesophageal Cancer — Score 6/6

- **Journal**: N Engl J Med
- **Pub date**: 2026-05-28
- **Authors**: Shitara K et al.
- **Aims**: To compare zanidatamab (bispecific HER2 antibody) ± tislelizumab plus chemotherapy versus trastuzumab plus chemotherapy as first-line therapy for HER2-positive advanced gastroesophageal adenocarcinoma.
- **Method**: Open-label phase 3 RCT (HERIZON-GEA-01); n=914; 1:1:1 randomisation; co-primary endpoints PFS and OS.
- **Key findings**: PFS longer with both zanidatamab regimens vs trastuzumab (HR 0.63 and 0.65; p<0.001 each). OS improved with zanidatamab-tislelizumab-chemo vs trastuzumab-chemo (median 26.4 vs 19.2 months; HR 0.72).
- **Strengths**: Large multinational phase 3 RCT with pre-specified dual primary endpoints and >25-month median follow-up.
- **Limitations**: OS for zanidatamab-alone arm was non-significant at interim analysis; grade ≥3 AE rate was 83% in the triplet arm.
- **Conclusion**: Zanidatamab-tislelizumab-chemotherapy significantly extends PFS and OS over trastuzumab-chemotherapy in HER2-positive gastroesophageal cancer.
- **ID**: DOI:10.1056/NEJMoa2517729 / PMID:42202319

## 2. Consistent Metabolomic and Genetic Evidence Supports Acisoga as a Key Contributor to Colorectal Neoplasia Progression — Score 6/6

- **Journal**: Cancer Epidemiol Biomarkers Prev
- **Pub date**: 2026-05-27
- **Authors**: Rius-Sansalvador B et al.
- **Aims**: To identify serum metabolites associated with CRC progression stages and validate causality through multi-omics integration and external replication.
- **Method**: Untargeted metabolomics (n=513, five lesion groups); metabolite-level GWAS; Mendelian randomisation; external validation in EPIC cohort (n=1,121 case-control pairs).
- **Key findings**: Acisoga (polyamine) showed a significant increasing trend across CRC stages (p=2.8×10⁻⁵). Validated in EPIC (OR=1.23; 95% CI 1.01–1.50); MR confirmed causal association (p=0.003).
- **Strengths**: Triangulated evidence across discovery metabolomics, GWAS, MR, and an independent prospective cohort strengthens causal inference.
- **Limitations**: MR effect estimates were modest; observational components retain residual confounding risk.
- **Conclusion**: Acisoga is a reproducible, genetically supported circulating biomarker candidate for CRC risk, warranting evaluation in screening populations.
- **ID**: DOI:10.1158/1055-9965.EPI-25-2050 / PMID:42201318

## 3. Very Low-Carbohydrate Ketogenic Diet in Treatment-Naïve Women with Endometrial Cancer and Overweight: A Randomized Feasibility Study — Score 5/6

- **Journal**: Nat Commun
- **Pub date**: 2026-05-27
- **Authors**: Dantas E et al.
- **Aims**: To evaluate feasibility, metabolic effects, and tumour immune microenvironment changes of a pre-surgical very-low-carbohydrate diet (VLCD) in overweight/obese endometrial cancer patients.
- **Method**: Multicenter prospective RCT (NCT03285152); n=19; 2:1 allocation to VLCD or standard diet for 21–28 days; RNA-Seq and IHC for tumour immune endpoints.
- **Key findings**: 91% meal adherence; 5.5% weight loss; fasting insulin fell 60%. VLCD was associated with CD8+ T-cell enrichment at tumour margins (q=0.034); no grade 3/4 adverse events.
- **Strengths**: First RCT to link short-term VLCD with tumour immune microenvironment changes in endometrial cancer, bridging diet and cancer biology.
- **Limitations**: Small sample (n=15 completers); short pre-surgical window precludes survival endpoint assessment.
- **Conclusion**: Short-term VLCD is feasible and well-tolerated pre-surgery in endometrial cancer; larger confirmatory trials are warranted.
- **ID**: DOI:10.1038/s41467-026-73519-w / PMID:42192131

## 4. The Analysis of Cancer Screening Trials in Which the Outcome Is the Incidence of Late-Stage Disease — Score 5/6

- **Journal**: Eur J Epidemiol
- **Pub date**: 2026-05-27
- **Authors**: Weiss NS et al.
- **Aims**: To identify and correct a methodological flaw in the analysis of the NHS-Galleri multi-cancer early detection RCT regarding exclusion of prevalent late-stage cases.
- **Method**: Methodological letter; formal epidemiological argument demonstrating asymmetric prevalent-case exclusion introduces bias; proposes symmetric exclusion strategy with stated assumptions.
- **Key findings**: Excluding prevalent stage IV cases only from the intervention arm—without counterpart exclusion in controls—inflates the apparent screening benefit on late-stage incidence.
- **Strengths**: Timely critique of a high-profile live RCT with a clearly actionable correction; broadly applicable to multi-cancer early detection trials.
- **Limitations**: Published as a letter without empirical data; magnitude of resulting bias in the Galleri results is not quantified.
- **Conclusion**: Valid screening trial analyses of late-stage cancer incidence require symmetric exclusion of prevalent advanced cases across both trial arms.
- **ID**: DOI:10.1007/s10654-026-01403-4 / PMID:42201458

## 5. No Smoker Left Behind: Evaluation of a Population-Based, Opt-Out Smoking Cessation Program for Patients with Cancer Who Smoke — Score 4/6

- **Journal**: J Clin Oncol
- **Pub date**: 2026-05-27
- **Authors**: Wakeman M et al.
- **Aims**: To evaluate reach, treatment uptake, and quit rates of a proactive opt-out smoking cessation outreach programme in a socioeconomically and racially diverse cancer patient population.
- **Method**: Single-institution implementation study (University of Chicago, 2019–2024); 3,706 eligible patients contacted via IVR/text/email; self-reported ≥8-day abstinence at 180 days; cost-outcome analysis.
- **Key findings**: 16.2% abstinence at day 180 among those with follow-up; costs $415/patient referred and $6,067/quit. Black patients had higher enrolment but lower abstinence rates than White patients (14.4% vs 25.7%).
- **Strengths**: Real-world scalable opt-out model with cost data in a diverse, publicly insured oncology population.
- **Limitations**: Only 37.5% completed 180-day follow-up; single institution; abstinence self-reported without biochemical verification.
- **Conclusion**: Opt-out proactive cessation programmes are feasible and cost-quantifiable in oncology, but racial disparities in outcomes require targeted intervention.
- **ID**: DOI:10.1200/JCO-25-02029 / PMID:42202244
