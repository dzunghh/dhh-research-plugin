## 1. Electronic Cigarette Use After Smoking Cessation and Lung Cancer Risk — Score 6/6

- **Journal**: Nature Medicine
- **Pub date**: 2026-06-08
- **Authors**: Kim YW et al.
- **Aims**: Evaluate whether e-cigarette use after smoking cessation is associated with lung cancer incidence and lung-cancer-specific death.
- **Method**: Retrospective cohort of 4,524,895 adults in the Korean National Health Screening Program (baseline 2018, follow-up to Dec 2023); multivariable Cox models stratified by cessation duration.
- **Key findings**: Post-cessation e-cig use associated with 56% higher lung cancer incidence (aHR 1.56, 95% CI 1.24–1.97) and doubled lung-cancer-specific death (aHR 2.00, 95% CI 1.28–3.15) vs complete quitters.
- **Strengths**: National registry, >24 million person-years, pre-stratified high-risk subgroup analysis consistent with main results.
- **Limitations**: Observational; single baseline e-cig exposure measure; causality cannot be established.
- **Conclusion**: E-cigarette use post-cessation may substantially attenuate the lung cancer benefit of quitting conventional smoking.
- **ID**: DOI:10.1038/s41591-026-04469-5 / PMID:42260103

## 2. First-Line Durvalumab Plus Trastuzumab Deruxtecan in HR-Negative HER2-Low Breast Cancer (BEGONIA Arm 6) — Score 5/6

- **Journal**: Nature Cancer
- **Pub date**: 2026-06-08
- **Authors**: Schmid P et al.
- **Aims**: Assess efficacy and safety of durvalumab plus T-DXd as first-line therapy for locally advanced/metastatic HR-negative HER2-low breast cancer.
- **Method**: Multicenter open-label phase 1b/2 BEGONIA platform trial; 58 women; T-DXd 5.4 mg/kg + durvalumab 1,120 mg IV q3w; median follow-up 20.6 months.
- **Key findings**: ORR 62.1% (95% CI 48.4–74.5); median PFS 12.6 months; median OS 30.3 months; ILD/pneumonitis in 20.7% (grade 5 in 1.7%).
- **Strengths**: Novel combination in a population with limited first-line options; prospective adaptive platform design.
- **Limitations**: Single-arm, n=58, no comparator; primary ORR endpoint not met (target 66.6%); ILD signal warrants monitoring.
- **Conclusion**: Durvalumab plus T-DXd demonstrated clinically relevant efficacy for first-line HR-negative HER2-low metastatic breast cancer with a manageable safety profile.
- **ID**: DOI:10.1038/s43018-026-01181-8 / PMID:42259988

## 3. GLP-1 Receptor Agonist Use and Cancer Risk in Obese Nondiabetic Adults — Score 5/6

- **Journal**: Annals of Oncology
- **Pub date**: 2026-06-07
- **Authors**: Hsu AH et al.
- **Aims**: Investigate whether GLP-1RA use reduces 13 obesity-associated cancer types in obese adults without diabetes.
- **Method**: Target trial emulation using TriNetX (113M-patient US database); 1:1 propensity score matching (n=161,798) of GLP-1RA vs diet/exercise counselling; IPTW validation; median 2-year follow-up.
- **Key findings**: GLP-1RA associated with 41% lower obesity-associated cancer incidence (HR 0.59, 95% CI 0.53–0.67); effect consistent across sexes and BMI strata but attenuated in Black patients.
- **Strengths**: Large real-world cohort, dual causal-inference validation (PSM + IPTW), subgroup analyses across sex, BMI, race, and drug type.
- **Limitations**: Short median follow-up (2 years); observational; racial disparity finding requires replication.
- **Conclusion**: GLP-1RA use linked to significantly lower short-term obesity-associated cancer incidence in obese nondiabetic adults.
- **ID**: DOI:10.1016/j.annonc.2026.04.013 / PMID:42252247

## 4. RenalCLIP: A Disease-Centric Vision-Language Foundation Model for Precision Oncology in Kidney Cancer — Score 5/6

- **Journal**: Nature Communications
- **Pub date**: 2026-06-08
- **Authors**: Tao Y et al.
- **Aims**: Develop and validate a domain-specific vision-language foundation model for non-invasive renal mass assessment.
- **Method**: Two-stage pre-training on 27,866 CT scans from 8,809 patients across multi-centre cohorts; benchmarked on 10 clinical tasks spanning diagnosis, anatomy, and survival prediction.
- **Key findings**: RenalCLIP outperformed general-purpose foundation models on all clinical tasks; achieved peak performance with only 20% training data; demonstrated zero-shot diagnostic capability.
- **Strengths**: Large multi-centre dataset, comprehensive clinical task coverage, strong data-efficiency results.
- **Limitations**: CT-modality only; no prospective external validation; clinical deployment pathway not established.
- **Conclusion**: Disease-centric pre-training yields a generalisable, data-efficient AI tool to enhance diagnostic precision and prognostic stratification in kidney cancer.
- **ID**: DOI:10.1038/s41467-026-74175-w / PMID:42259830

## 5. Ultra-Processed Foods and Gastrointestinal Cancer: Epidemiologic Evidence, Mechanistic Pathways, and Clinical Implications — Score 3/6

- **Journal**: Cancer Epidemiology, Biomarkers & Prevention
- **Pub date**: 2026-06-08
- **Authors**: Tai D et al.
- **Aims**: Synthesise epidemiologic evidence and mechanistic data linking ultra-processed food (UPF) intake to GI malignancies.
- **Method**: Narrative review integrating observational studies and experimental data for colorectal, gastric, esophageal, and pancreatic cancers using NOVA classification.
- **Key findings**: High UPF intake associated with 10–30% higher colorectal cancer risk; suggestive associations for non-cardia gastric and esophageal adenocarcinoma; pancreatic evidence inconclusive.
- **Strengths**: Integrates dose-response epidemiologic data with mechanistic pathways (gut microbiome, IGF signalling, inflammation, food-matrix disruption).
- **Limitations**: Narrative not systematic review; heterogeneous UPF definitions; no pooled quantitative estimates.
- **Conclusion**: Converging observational and mechanistic evidence suggests UPF reduction may be a viable GI cancer prevention strategy.
- **ID**: DOI:10.1158/1055-9965.EPI-26-0417 / PMID:42257587
