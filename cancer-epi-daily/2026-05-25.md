## 1. Systemic Glucocorticoid Immunosuppression and Survival Among Immune Checkpoint Inhibitor Recipients — Score 6/6

- **Journal**: JAMA Network Open
- **Pub date**: 2026-05-01
- **Authors**: Wan G et al.
- **Aims**: Examine associations of glucocorticoid timing, dose, and duration with overall survival in ICI recipients.
- **Method**: Retrospective cohort (n=39,258); propensity-matched MGBD + TriNetX validation; accelerated failure time models.
- **Key findings**: Glucocorticoids within 1 month of ICI initiation associated with TR 0.49 (95% CI, 0.45–0.54); doses >60 mg/d prednisone-equivalent linked to 37% shorter OS.
- **Strengths**: Large multi-institution cohort independently validated in TriNetX database; dose and duration granularity.
- **Limitations**: Retrospective design with residual confounding by indication; no causal inference adjustment.
- **Conclusion**: Glucocorticoid use near or during ICI therapy is strongly and dose-dependently associated with shorter survival across cancer types.
- **ID**: DOI:10.1001/jamanetworkopen.2026.14323 / PMID:42172027

## 2. A Bayesian Bivariate Spatial Modeling Framework for Stage-Specific Cancer Incidence and Targeting of Screening Efforts — Score 6/6

- **Journal**: American Journal of Epidemiology
- **Pub date**: 2026-05-19
- **Authors**: Lin Q et al.
- **Aims**: Develop a Bayesian bivariate spatial model jointly estimating early- and late-stage cancer incidence to prioritise under-screened neighbourhoods.
- **Method**: Bayesian bivariate BYM2 spatial model via INLA; Swedish population-based prostate cancer registry at small-area level.
- **Key findings**: Bivariate model outperforms single-outcome formulations; late-minus-early difference identifies priority sub-areas where screening yield is highest.
- **Strengths**: Novel inferential framework; combines spatial and outcome correlation; extendable to other cancers.
- **Limitations**: Demonstrated only for prostate cancer in Sweden; temporal pooling of cross-sectional data.
- **Conclusion**: Bayesian bivariate spatial modelling provides a principled, reproducible tool for targeting cancer screening efforts at the sub-area level.
- **ID**: DOI:10.1093/aje/kwag106 / PMID:42154523

## 3. Development and Validation of an Epidemiological Risk Score for Early Detection of Pancreatic Cancer in High-Risk Individuals Within the IMAGene Project — Score 6/6

- **Journal**: International Journal of Epidemiology
- **Pub date**: 2026-04-17 (EDAT: 2026-05-19)
- **Authors**: Riobo-Mayo L et al.
- **Aims**: Validate a meta-analysis-derived epidemiological risk score (ERS) for PDAC against imaging and in a large biobank.
- **Method**: ERS derived from 24-factor meta-analysis; prospective cohort n=178 high-risk individuals (whole-body MRI); external validation in UK Biobank (n≈300,000; 1,648 PDAC cases).
- **Key findings**: Higher ERS associated with malignant pancreatic lesions on MRI (P=0.01); extra-pancreatic malignant lesions also more frequent (P=0.02); UK Biobank validation confirmed.
- **Strengths**: Multi-stage validation (prospective cohort + UK Biobank); low-cost pre-imaging screening tool.
- **Limitations**: Small initial validation cohort (n=178); single imaging modality; no biomarker integration.
- **Conclusion**: The ERS is a feasible, low-cost risk stratification tool that could guide earlier MRI-based PDAC surveillance in high-risk individuals.
- **ID**: DOI:10.1093/ije/dyag064 / PMID:42160519

## 4. Bowel Cancer Care in Individuals With an Intellectual Disability: A Population-Based Cohort Study of Symptoms, Diagnostic Pathways, Treatment and Survival — Score 6/6

- **Journal**: BMC Medicine
- **Pub date**: 2026-05-20
- **Authors**: Kennedy OJ et al.
- **Aims**: Characterise the full bowel cancer care pathway (symptoms to survival) in people with intellectual disability (ID) versus matched controls.
- **Method**: CPRD-linked matched cohort; 111,034 individuals with ID vs 1,964,420 controls; Cox, Poisson, and modified Poisson regression.
- **Key findings**: ID associated with 30% higher bowel cancer risk (aHR 1.30, 1.18–1.44); 57% less likely to receive USC referral (aRR 0.57); 2× higher bowel cancer-specific mortality (aHR 2.00, 1.71–2.33).
- **Strengths**: Largest linked-data study of cancer care in ID; entire pathway analysis from presentation to survival.
- **Limitations**: UK-only; ID severity unmeasured; possible residual confounding by comorbidity.
- **Conclusion**: People with intellectual disability face systematic inequities across the entire bowel cancer care pathway, supporting earlier and more intensive screening strategies.
- **ID**: DOI:10.1186/s12916-026-04906-9 / PMID:42157251

## 5. A Composite Low-Grade Inflammation Score and Cancer Outcomes: Results from the Moli-sani Study — Score 5/6

- **Journal**: Cancer Epidemiology, Biomarkers & Prevention
- **Pub date**: 2026-05-19
- **Authors**: Costanzo S et al.
- **Aims**: Investigate whether the INFLA-score (CRP + granulocyte-to-lymphocyte ratio + leukocyte + platelet counts) predicts cancer mortality, hospitalisation, and colorectal cancer.
- **Method**: Prospective cohort n=18,933 (51.8% women; ≥35 years); 13.1-year follow-up; multivariable Cox proportional hazards models.
- **Key findings**: Highest vs lower INFLA-score fifths: cancer hospitalisation HR 1.21 (1.08–1.34); colorectal cancer HR 1.68 (1.30–2.18); cancer mortality HR 1.19 (95% CI crosses 1.0).
- **Strengths**: Long follow-up, large prospective cohort; three independent cancer outcomes; population-based.
- **Limitations**: Single Italian cohort limits generalisability; cancer mortality CI marginally crosses null; no external validation of INFLA-score.
- **Conclusion**: A composite low-grade inflammation score independently predicts colorectal cancer incidence and cancer hospitalisation, supporting systemic inflammation monitoring in prevention.
- **ID**: DOI:10.1158/1055-9965.EPI-26-0312 / PMID:42154253

## Appraisal notes

This week's picks reflect a methodological turn toward integrating spatial and composite-score approaches in cancer epidemiology: both the Bayesian bivariate small-area estimation framework (Am J Epidemiol) and the PDAC risk score validated in UK Biobank illustrate the field's push toward actionable, scalable risk stratification tools. The pharmacoepidemiological study of glucocorticoids and ICI survival (JAMA Netw Open, n=39,258 across two independent cohorts) stands out for its clinical immediacy and rigorous validation design. The intellectual disability bowel cancer cohort underscores persistent healthcare inequity as an epi priority, while the Moli-sani INFLA-score study advances the hypothesis that routine blood-count biomarkers can proxy cancer risk at population scale.
