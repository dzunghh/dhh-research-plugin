## 1. Possible selection bias in Mendelian randomization studies: effect of body mass index on breast cancer as an example — Score 6/6

- **Journal**: American Journal of Epidemiology
- **Pub date**: 2026-06-04
- **Authors**: Fei K et al.
- **Aims**: Investigate whether survivorship-driven selection bias in GWAS explains the paradoxical inverse BMI–postmenopausal breast cancer association in MR studies.
- **Method**: Cross-GWAS comparison using BCAC consortium, UK Biobank, and sibling breast cancer GWAS summary statistics with MR analysis.
- **Key findings**: Higher genetically predicted BMI inversely associated with BC in BCAC (OR 0.74) and UK Biobank (OR 0.70), but no association in sibling BC GWAS (OR 1.00, 95% CI 0.99–1.01). Suggests the discrepancy is selection-bias artefact.
- **Strengths**: Three independent GWAS used; sibling design provides a bias-free reference, triangulating the finding.
- **Limitations**: Relies on summary statistics; cannot rule out other sources of heterogeneity across cohorts.
- **Conclusion**: MR studies may suffer survivorship selection bias when both exposure and outcome elevate premature mortality, calling for routine bias-sensitivity checks.
- **ID**: DOI:10.1093/aje/kwag125 / PMID:42237572

## 2. Risk Factors for Subsequent Primary Cancers in Colorectal Cancer Survivors: A Systematic Review and Meta-analysis — Score 6/6

- **Journal**: Cancer Epidemiology, Biomarkers & Prevention
- **Pub date**: 2026-06-04
- **Authors**: Aung YK et al.
- **Aims**: Synthesise associations between treatment, tumour features, and lifestyle factors with subsequent primary cancer (SPC) risk in colorectal cancer survivors.
- **Method**: Systematic review of 36 studies (10,577 screened); random-effects meta-analysis of pooled relative risks for each SPC type.
- **Key findings**: Radiotherapy raised uterine (RR 2.65), ovarian (RR 2.26), and urethral cancer risk (RR 4.66); chemotherapy in women raised uterine (RR 2.26) and breast cancer risk (RR 1.22). Older age, smoking, and metabolic comorbidities also elevated SPC risk.
- **Strengths**: Broad cancer-site coverage across 36 studies; random-effects models account for between-study heterogeneity.
- **Limitations**: Heterogeneous follow-up durations and treatment regimens across included studies limit precision of pooled estimates.
- **Conclusion**: Treatment- and lifestyle-related SPC risk factors identified support risk-stratified post-CRC surveillance protocols.
- **ID**: DOI:10.1158/1055-9965.EPI-26-0386 / PMID:42240466

## 3. PD-(L)1 Inhibitor Monotherapy vs Chemoimmunotherapy for Advanced NSCLC With High PD-L1 Expression: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis — Score 6/6

- **Journal**: JAMA Oncology
- **Pub date**: 2026-06-04
- **Authors**: Di Federico A et al.
- **Aims**: Compare overall and progression-free survival for PD-(L)1 monotherapy versus chemoimmunotherapy in treatment-naive advanced NSCLC with PD-L1 ≥50%.
- **Method**: Meta-analysis of 24 phase 3 RCTs (n=5,546); inverse-variance, network meta-analysis, and reconstructed individual patient data from Kaplan-Meier curves.
- **Key findings**: Chemoimmunotherapy conferred superior OS vs monotherapy (HR 0.85, 95% CI 0.73–0.99; median OS 29.2 vs 19.8 months) and markedly better PFS (HR 0.61, 95% CI 0.50–0.75).
- **Strengths**: Network meta-analysis and IPD reconstruction provide robust comparative evidence beyond head-to-head trials.
- **Limitations**: Absence of direct randomised comparison; cross-trial patient heterogeneity may confound indirect estimates.
- **Conclusion**: Chemoimmunotherapy significantly outperforms monotherapy in PD-L1-high advanced NSCLC, challenging the default monotherapy approach.
- **ID**: DOI:10.1001/jamaoncol.2026.1548 / PMID:42240993

## 4. Association of cultural racism with breast cancer survival among United States adults, 2018-2020 — Score 5/6

- **Journal**: American Journal of Epidemiology
- **Pub date**: 2026-06-04
- **Authors**: Gupta A et al.
- **Aims**: Estimate whether state-level cultural racism is independently associated with breast cancer mortality.
- **Method**: Cox proportional hazards models with state as random effect applied to SEER data (n=225,991); time-varying effect analysis and race-stratified subgroups.
- **Key findings**: Each unit increase in cultural racism factor (CRF) was associated with 8% higher death risk (aHR 1.08, 95% CI 1.05–1.11); effects were strongest early in follow-up (baseline HR 1.30).
- **Strengths**: Large national SEER sample; validated state-level multidimensional CRF measure; time-varying modelling.
- **Limitations**: Ecological CRF measure may not capture individual-level racism exposure; residual confounding possible.
- **Conclusion**: Cultural racism is an independent population-level predictor of breast cancer mortality, with time-dependent effects concentrated early post-diagnosis.
- **ID**: DOI:10.1093/aje/kwag126 / PMID:42237574

## 5. Individual-level trends in mammography screening over 14 years: results from an all-payer statewide claims database — Score 5/6

- **Journal**: Cancer Epidemiology, Biomarkers & Prevention
- **Pub date**: 2026-06-04
- **Authors**: Nowak SA et al.
- **Aims**: Characterise individual-level mammography screening adherence across 14 years spanning guideline changes and the COVID-19 pandemic.
- **Method**: Retrospective analysis of 2010–2023 Vermont all-payer insurance claims; biennial screening adherence tracked by birth cohort and age group.
- **Key findings**: Biennial mammography adherence fell 20.7 pp (ages 40–49) and 6.7 pp (ages 50–74) over the period; younger birth cohorts show systematically lower uptake; post-pandemic rates for <50 cohorts exceeded pre-pandemic levels.
- **Strengths**: Rare 14-year individual-level longitudinal claims data covering all payers; cohort and period effects disaggregated.
- **Limitations**: Single-state Vermont data limit generalisability; claims miss uninsured women.
- **Conclusion**: Long-term screening decline persists across cohorts, though pandemic disruption paradoxically boosted rates in younger women, a trend worth sustaining.
- **ID**: DOI:10.1158/1055-9965.EPI-26-0082 / PMID:42240455

## Appraisal notes

This week's digest is anchored by an important methodological warning: survivorship selection bias can invert MR findings for cancer risk factors sharing mortality pathways with the exposure (paper 1), a reminder that triangulation with bias-robust designs (e.g., sibling GWAS) is essential before drawing causal conclusions. Two papers focus on cancer survivorship epidemiology — subsequent primary cancer risk after colorectal cancer (paper 2) and mammography adherence trends (paper 5) — reflecting growing research attention to post-cancer surveillance and screening inequities. The cultural racism paper (paper 4) extends social-determinants epi into a validated structural construct, complementing the JAMA Oncology meta-analysis (paper 3) that resolves a prominent clinical-treatment question using robust comparative methods.
