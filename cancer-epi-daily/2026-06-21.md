## 1. Energy Balance-Related Factors and Direct Tumor-Adipocyte Contact in Colorectal Cancer: Etiology Insights from the Population-Based Netherlands Cohort Study — Score 6/6

- **Journal**: Cancer Epidemiol Biomarkers Prev
- **Pub date**: 2026-06-18
- **Authors**: Offermans K et al.
- **Aims**: To determine whether pre-diagnostic energy balance factors differentially predict SARIFA-positive versus SARIFA-negative colorectal cancer risk.
- **Method**: Molecular pathological epidemiology within the Netherlands Cohort Study (n=120,852); 1,826 CRC cases with SARIFA status; multivariable Cox regression.
- **Key findings**: BMI more strongly associated with SARIFA-positive colon cancer in men (HR per 5 kg/m²: 1.57, 95%CI 1.20–2.07) than SARIFA-negative (HR 1.22, 95%CI 1.01–1.48). Physical activity inversely linked to SARIFA-positive tumors in women.
- **Strengths**: Large population-based cohort with validated histopathologic biomarker; sex-stratified analyses.
- **Limitations**: SARIFA status not available for all cases; observational design limits causal inference.
- **Conclusion**: Adiposity preferentially drives SARIFA-positive colon cancer, linking body fatness to a specific tumor-stroma phenotype.
- **ID**: DOI:10.1158/1055-9965.EPI-26-0079 / PMID:42312939

## 2. Genetic and Health Determinants of Cancer Risk in Bangladeshi and Pakistani Individuals in the UK — Score 6/6

- **Journal**: Nat Commun
- **Pub date**: 2026-06-18
- **Authors**: Dayem Ullah AZM et al.
- **Aims**: To characterise cancer risk architecture in British Bangladeshi and Pakistani populations using linked genomic and health-record data.
- **Method**: Multi-modal analysis of 57,416 individuals in the Genes & Health cohort; EHR + cancer registry linkage + whole-exome sequencing (n=43,462); phenome-wide and exome-wide analyses.
- **Key findings**: 132 significant cancer-comorbidity associations identified; 39 variant-level and 31 gene-level cancer associations, >60% absent from major genomic databases; earlier cancer onset versus national benchmarks; novel loci include ARHGAP45, CBR1, NF1, and ZNF155.
- **Strengths**: Largest South Asian cancer genomics cohort to date; integrates EHR, registry, and WES in a single framework.
- **Limitations**: Restricted to UK Bangladeshi/Pakistani populations; generalisability to South Asian populations elsewhere uncertain.
- **Conclusion**: Distinct, largely catalogued cancer risk variants in this South Asian subpopulation demand population-tailored screening and risk models.
- **ID**: DOI:10.1038/s41467-026-74415-z / PMID:42315506

## 3. Prognostic Dynamics of Pathological Complete Response and ctDNA Clearance after Neoadjuvant/Perioperative Immunotherapy in Cancer: A Reconstructed Individual Patient Analysis — Score 5/6

- **Journal**: J Natl Cancer Inst
- **Pub date**: 2026-06-18
- **Authors**: Garitaonaindia Y et al.
- **Aims**: To compare the prognostic value of pCR and ctDNA clearance as surrogate endpoints across tumour types in neoadjuvant immunotherapy trials.
- **Method**: Reconstructed individual patient data meta-analysis from published Kaplan-Meier curves of phase II/III trials (8 tumour types; n=1,867 pCR, n=352 ctDNA clearance).
- **Key findings**: Three-year EFS among pCR patients ranged from 78.6% to 100% by tumour type; postoperative adjuvant IO did not improve EFS in pCR patients; ctDNA clearance showed heterogeneous prognostic dynamics.
- **Strengths**: Pan-tumour scope; reconstructed IPD permits individual-level survival analysis without raw data access.
- **Limitations**: Reconstructed data introduce bias; cross-trial heterogeneity in ctDNA assays limits comparability.
- **Conclusion**: pCR remains the most reproducible surrogate across tumour types; ctDNA clearance needs standardisation before use as a universal endpoint.
- **ID**: DOI:10.1093/jnci/djag197 / PMID:42317065

## 4. ypTNM versus pTNM Staging after Neoadjuvant Therapy in Rectal Cancer — Score 5/6

- **Journal**: J Natl Cancer Inst
- **Pub date**: 2026-06-17
- **Authors**: Miyakawa T et al.
- **Aims**: To determine whether ypTNM classification confers worse prognosis than pTNM at equivalent stages in rectal cancer.
- **Method**: Retrospective cohort using National Cancer Database (n=50,436); Kaplan-Meier and multivariable Cox regression stratified by guideline-concordant care.
- **Key findings**: Stage-for-stage ypTNM associated with significantly worse survival than pTNM: stage II HR 1.50 (95%CI 1.27–1.77), stage III HR 1.60 (95%CI 1.46–1.75). Divergence increased at higher stages.
- **Strengths**: Large national registry with long follow-up; stratification by guideline-concordant care addresses treatment selection bias.
- **Limitations**: Retrospective design; NCDB lacks detailed neoadjuvant regimen data; residual confounding likely.
- **Conclusion**: Staging systems should formally distinguish ypTNM from pTNM to improve prognostication and clinical trial stratification.
- **ID**: DOI:10.1093/jnci/djag192 / PMID:42308525

## 5. The Impact of Adherence to Cancer Prevention Recommendations on Cancer Incidence and Mortality in a Cohort of Canadian Adults — Score 5/6

- **Journal**: Cancer Epidemiol Biomarkers Prev
- **Pub date**: 2026-06-18
- **Authors**: Fiati Kenston SS et al.
- **Aims**: To quantify associations between adherence to WCRF/AICR cancer prevention recommendations and cancer incidence and mortality in a Canadian cohort.
- **Method**: Prospective cohort analysis; British Columbia Generations Project (n=16,053, 2009–2022); cancer registry linkage; composite WCRF/AICR adherence score (0–5); multivariable Cox models.
- **Key findings**: Full adherence versus non-adherence: cancer incidence HR 0.68 (95%CI 0.54–0.78); cancer mortality HR 0.66 (95%CI 0.49–0.88). Benefit seen for breast, colorectal, and lung cancers in both sexes.
- **Strengths**: Registry-linked prospective design with 13-year follow-up; sex-specific and cancer-type subgroup analyses; sensitivity analysis excluding early follow-up cancers.
- **Limitations**: Modest sample size limits power for rare cancers; self-reported behavioural exposures susceptible to misclassification.
- **Conclusion**: Multi-behaviour cancer prevention adherence substantially reduces cancer incidence and mortality in Canadian adults.
- **ID**: DOI:10.1158/1055-9965.EPI-26-0590 / PMID:42312951

## Appraisal notes

This week's cohort reflects a convergence of molecular pathological epidemiology and genomic equity: the two highest-scoring papers integrate biobank-scale genomics with real-world health records to reveal differential cancer risk by adiposity phenotype and by ancestry (Bangladeshi/Pakistani populations remain dramatically underrepresented in global genomic databases). The two JNCI papers together push on clinical staging methodology and surrogate-endpoint validity in immunotherapy trials, both finding that current conventions overestimate prognostic homogeneity. Collectively, these papers signal growing methodological interest in disaggregating 'cancer' into molecularly or biologically distinct subtypes before drawing epidemiological conclusions.
