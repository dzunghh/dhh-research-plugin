# Vietnamese Epidemiology Context

## When to Apply This File

Apply this guidance whenever:
- The user writes in Vietnamese
- The user mentions Vietnamese datasets, hospitals, or health facilities
- The question involves Vietnamese disease patterns or risk factors
- The user references MOH (Bộ Y tế) guidelines or Vietnamese regulations
- The study population is Vietnamese or the data is from Vietnam

Respond in Vietnamese when the user writes in Vietnamese. Use Vietnamese terminology consistently throughout the response.

## Vietnamese Academic Register

Vietnamese academic writing is more formal than English equivalents. Key differences:

**Pronouns:**
- Methods section: Avoid "chúng tôi" (we) — use passive constructions or "nghiên cứu này" (this study)
- Discussion: "chúng tôi cho rằng" (we believe/suggest) is acceptable
- Introduction: avoid first-person until Discussion

**Hedging:**
Vietnamese academic writing hedges more conservatively than English:
- "có thể" (may/might)
- "dường như" (appears to)
- "có khả năng" (is likely)
- "phát hiện này gợi ý rằng" (this finding suggests that)
- "cần thêm nghiên cứu để..." (further research is needed to...)

**Citation style:** Vietnamese journals often use numeric format [1] rather than (Author, Year). Confirm target journal style.

**Passive constructions:** More heavy use of passive voice throughout, especially in Methods and Results.

## Vietnamese Healthcare System

### Three-Tier Structure
Vietnam's public health system operates in three tiers — always consider this when discussing generalizability, selection bias, and study population:

1. **Commune-level** (Trạm y tế xã): Primary care, vaccination, maternal health, chronic disease management — mostly rural
2. **District-level** (Bệnh viện huyện): Secondary care, general hospitals
3. **Provincial / Central** (Bệnh viện tỉnh / Trung ương): Tertiary care, specialist centers, where most cancer diagnoses are made

**Implications for research:**
- Hospital-based studies usually recruit from provincial/central hospitals → severity bias (sicker patients)
- Cancer incidence data from hospital records underestimates true incidence
- Rural-urban differences in healthcare access create selection pressures
- North-South differences: distinct genetic backgrounds, dietary patterns, occupational exposures, healthcare infrastructure

### Key Regulatory Bodies
- **Ministry of Health (Bộ Y tế — BYT):** Oversees all medical research, sets ethical standards
- **Institutional Review Board (Hội đồng đạo đức):** Required approval for all human subjects research
- **Vietnam Cancer Registry Network:** Provides population-based cancer incidence data; coverage varies by province
- **General Statistics Office (Tổng cục Thống kê):** Population and vital statistics data

## Vietnamese Epidemiology Priorities

### High-Burden Diseases

**Hepatocellular Carcinoma (HCC) / Ung thư biểu mô tế bào gan:**
- Vietnam has one of the world's highest liver cancer incidence rates
- Hepatitis B (HBV) seroprevalence ~8–10%; Hepatitis C (HCV) ~2–3%
- HBV vaccination program began 1997 (mandatory since 2003) — consider cohort effects
- HBV status is a critical confounder in ALL liver-related studies; never omit
- Aflatoxin contamination of stored rice/corn is an additional risk factor

**Lung Cancer / Ung thư phổi:**
- Male smoking prevalence ~45% (declining); female <2%
- Unexpectedly high rates in non-smoking women — EGFR mutations, indoor air pollution (cooking fumes), radon exposure in northern highlands

**Cervical Cancer / Ung thư cổ tử cung:**
- High burden; HPV vaccination program expanding (not yet universal)
- Cervical screening coverage remains suboptimal outside urban areas

**Gastric Cancer / Ung thư dạ dày:**
- H. pylori prevalence ~70% — a key confounder
- High salt diet, pickled foods as additional exposures

**Stroke / Đột quỵ:**
- Leading cause of death and disability
- Hemorrhagic stroke proportion higher than in Western populations

**TB / Lao:**
- Vietnam is among 30 high-TB-burden countries
- Drug-resistant TB (MDR-TB) increasing

### Vietnam-Specific Exposures

**Agent Orange / Chất độc da cam:**
- Dioxin exposure from herbicide use during the Vietnam War (1961–1971)
- Affects provinces in central and southern Vietnam (Đà Nẵng, Biên Hòa hotspots)
- Associated with soft tissue sarcomas, NHL, Hodgkin lymphoma, multiple myeloma, type 2 diabetes
- If studying these conditions in affected provinces, consider as potential unmeasured exposure

**Occupational hazards:**
- Agriculture (pesticide exposure, sun/heat, infectious agents)
- Manufacturing (textile, electronics — chemical exposures)
- Mining (coal, bauxite — respiratory hazards)
- Street food vendors (air pollution, heat)

**Dietary patterns:**
- Transitioning from traditional (rice-based, low meat, fermented vegetables) to Western diet — important in time-trend studies
- Nuoc mam (fish sauce) — high salt content
- Betel nut chewing — oral cancer risk, particularly in older generations and ethnic minorities
- Traditional herbal medicine use — relevant in pharmacoepidemiology and drug interaction studies

## Practical Tips for Vietnamese Research

**Ethics and consent:**
- Two levels often required: institutional ethics committee + Ministry of Health
- Informed consent forms must be in Vietnamese; culturally appropriate language
- Community leaders may need to be involved for rural/ethnic minority studies

**Data quality:**
- ICD coding quality varies by hospital tier and year
- Death certificates have incomplete cause-of-death data in many provinces
- Consider data validation substudy (medical record review) for primary outcomes

**Language in publications:**
- Many Vietnamese journals require Vietnamese abstract + English abstract
- Major international publications expect English; consider local journals for Vietnamese health data that are primarily relevant to national policy
- TCMR (Tạp chí Y học dự phòng) and VJPH (Vietnam Journal of Preventive Medicine) are key domestic epi journals

**Generalizability statements:**
When discussing generalizability from Vietnamese hospital-based studies:
"Nghiên cứu được thực hiện tại bệnh viện tuyến trung ương, do đó tính đại diện cho người bệnh tại tuyến cơ sở có thể bị hạn chế. Cần thêm các nghiên cứu dựa vào cộng đồng để xác nhận kết quả này."
(The study was conducted at a central-level hospital; representativeness for community-level patients may be limited. Community-based studies are needed to confirm these findings.)
